==================

The Most Radical Voice of Liberalism
------------------------------------

Hans-Hermann Hoppe (1949- ) is a German-born economist and political
philosopher who has justified private property rights and liberalism a
priori through argumentation ethics. As a student and intellectual
heir of Rothbard, he occupies a unique position in contemporary
liberal thought through his radical critique of democracy and his
social theory grounded in a private property order.

Hoppe's works are controversial, but both his supporters and critics
acknowledge the logical rigor of his arguments.

Biography
---------

## From Germany to America

Hoppe was born in 1949 in Peine, West Germany. He studied philosophy,
sociology, and economics at Saarland University, Goethe University
(Frankfurt), and the University of Michigan. While at Frankfurt, he
earned his doctoral degree under Jürgen Habermas.

This is a noteworthy intellectual background. Habermas is a leading
figure of the Frankfurt School and a master of left-wing social
theory. Hoppe drew inspiration from Habermas's discourse ethics, but
applied it to reach the opposite conclusions - namely, the
justification of private property and the illegitimacy of the state.
He defended liberalism with a tool from the left.

In 1986, Hoppe immigrated to the United States and served as a
professor of economics at the University of Nevada, Las Vegas (UNLV).
Meeting Rothbard at the same university formed a decisive intellectual
alliance. Rothbard described Hoppe's argumentation ethics as "the most
interesting justification of liberalism."

After retiring from the university in 2006, he moved through Turkey
and now resides in Istanbul, where he founded the Property and Freedom
Society and hosts annual academic conferences.

Core Ideas
----------

## Argumentation Ethics

This is Hoppe's most original contribution. The core argument proceeds
as follows:

Premise: Consider the very act of argumentation itself - discussing
what norms are justified. What do the two people engaged in
argumentation presuppose?

* The Presupposition of Self-Ownership: To participate in
argumentation means to freely use one's own body (brain, vocal cords,
hands). If the other party had control over my body, I could not
participate in argumentation.

* The Presupposition of Non-Aggression: Argumentation is an activity
aimed at persuading the other through logic and evidence, not through
physical coercion. To justify force while simultaneously engaging in
argumentation is a performative contradiction.

* The Presupposition of Private Property: If we grant self-ownership,
we logically derive the right to property in external resources
transformed by one's labor (homesteading).

Therefore: Any norm that denies private property rights contradicts
argumentation itself. Private property rights are the only justifiable
norms.

The core strength of this argument is that it is a priori. It does not
depend on empirical data or subjective value judgments, but derives
directly from the logical structure of the activity of argumentation.

## Critique of Democracy: Why Democracy Is a Failed God

Hoppe's most controversial work is Democracy: The God That Failed
(2001).

Hoppe compares democracy and monarchy from the perspective of
protecting private property. The core argument:

A monarch (king) regards the state as his own private property.
Therefore, he has an incentive to preserve the value of the state (its
capital value) in the long term. Since the king will pass the state to
his descendants, excessive expropriation would diminish the value of
his property.

A democratic ruler (president, congressman) merely leases the state
temporarily. Once his term ends, he must leave, so he has no incentive
to preserve capital value. Instead, he has an incentive to extract
(expropriate) as much as possible during his term. This is the
structural reason for endless increases in government spending and
debt under democracy.

Hoppe is not defending monarchy in this comparison. His conclusion is
that any form of state violates private property, and the true
alternative is a private property order, that is, a stateless society.

## Time Preference and Civilization

Hoppe extends time preference theory into a theory of civilization.
Low time preference [/ideas/time-preference/] - the tendency to forego
present satisfaction for the future - enables saving, investment, and
long-term planning, and is the foundation of civilizational
development.

According to Hoppe, the state (particularly the democratic state)
artificially increases time preference:

* Inflation destroys the value of savings, signaling to consume
immediately
* High taxes reduce the rewards for future investment
* The welfare state allows present consumption without personal
responsibility
* Public debt transfers costs to future generations

Civilizational decline is a consequence of high time preference, and
state expansion is its cause.

## Private Law Society

The alternative social order presented by Hoppe is a private law
society. In this society:

* Dispute Resolution: Provided competitively by private arbitration
agencies
* Public Order: Provided by private security companies and insurance
companies
* Defense: Armed citizens and private defense associations
* Infrastructure: Everything provided in markets based on private
property

Insurance companies play a crucial role in this vision. They have
economic interest in protecting their customers' property, and thus
have incentives to invest efficiently in crime prevention and dispute
resolution.

Property and Freedom Society
----------------------------

Founded by Hoppe in 2006, this society hosts annual academic
conferences in Bodrum, Turkey. Libertarian scholars, economists, and
philosophers from around the world gather to conduct scholarly
discussions on the state, property rights, and freedom, aiming to
provide a free intellectual space removed from censorship and
self-censorship in mainstream academia.

Major Works
-----------

* A Theory of Socialism and Capitalism (1989) - systematic critique of
various forms of socialism
* The Economics and Ethics of Private Property (1993)
* Democracy: The God That Failed (2001) - the representative work on
the critique of democracy
* The Great Fiction (2012) - a collection of essays
