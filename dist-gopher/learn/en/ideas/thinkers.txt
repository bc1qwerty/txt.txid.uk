Thinkers Who Defended Freedom and the Market
============================================

2026-03-05 | en

A bird's-eye view of 200 years of the Austrian School's intellectual lineage - from Bastiat to Rothbard, including Böhm-Bawerk and Hazlitt.

Why the Intellectual Lineage Matters
------------------------------------

Austrian Economics is not a discipline created from textbooks. It is
an intellectual tradition accumulated over 200 years, as one
generation of thinkers passed core insights to the next. Learning
individual theories without understanding this tradition is like
seeing only the trees while missing the forest.

In the introductory course, you encountered concepts like Subjective
Value Theory [/ideas/subjective-value/], Marginal Utility
[/ideas/marginal-utility/], and Business Cycle Theory [/ideas/abct/].
In this intermediate course, you will meet the people who created
those concepts in chronological order. By tracing who first proposed
what and how the next thinker built on it, you can finally understand
why each theory took the shape it did.

200 Years of Intellectual Lineage
---------------------------------

## Bastiat: See the Unseen

Frédéric Bastiat (1801-1850) [/people/frederic-bastiat/] was a French
economist who preceded the Austrian School but is considered its
intellectual ancestor. In his essay "That Which Is Seen, and That
Which Is Not Seen," Bastiat argued that when evaluating economic
policy, we must consider not just visible effects but also the unseen
opportunity costs. This insight forms the methodological foundation of
the Austrian School.

## Menger: The Birth of a School

Carl Menger (1840-1921) [/people/carl-menger/] founded the Austrian
School with the publication of Principles of Economics in 1871. His
central contribution was the Subjective Value Theory: the value of a
thing does not reside in the thing itself but arises from the
subjective judgment of the individual using it. This was a revolution
that fundamentally overturned the labor theory of value.

## Böhm-Bawerk: Capital and Time

Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk (1851-1914) [/people/eugen-bohm-bawerk/] was a
student of Menger who extended the Subjective Value Theory into a
theory of capital and interest. He explained the essence of capital
through the concept of roundabout production and revealed the source
of interest through Time Preference. He is also famous for his
decisive critique of Marx's exploitation theory. Mises emerged from
his Vienna seminar, making him the critical bridge between Menger and
Mises.

## Mises: Completing the System

Ludwig von Mises (1881-1973) [/people/ludwig-von-mises/] expanded
Menger's Subjective Value Theory into the vast framework of praxeology
[/ideas/praxeology/] - deriving all economic laws deductively from the
axiom that "humans act." He also demonstrated through the Economic
Calculation Problem [/ideas/economic-calculation/] that socialism is
fundamentally unable to function.

## Hayek: Knowledge and Order

Friedrich Hayek (1899-1992) [/people/friedrich-hayek/] was a student
of Mises who added the decisive insight of "dispersed knowledge." The
knowledge required for an economy is so widely dispersed that no
central authority can collect it; only the price mechanism can
transmit this knowledge efficiently. Through the concept of
Spontaneous Order [/ideas/spontaneous-order/], he explained the
principle by which markets, language, and law form order without
design.

## Rothbard: Integrating Ethics and Economics

Murray Rothbard (1926-1995) [/people/murray-rothbard/] integrated
Mises's economics with natural rights ethics to create the coherent
system of anarcho-capitalism [/ideas/anarcho-capitalism/]. He argued
that the very existence of the state violates the Non-Aggression
Principle [/ideas/non-aggression-principle/], pushing libertarianism
to its most logical conclusion.

Key Figures
-----------

* Frédéric Bastiat [/people/frederic-bastiat/] - Pioneer who taught us
how to see the unseen
* Carl Menger [/people/carl-menger/] - Founded the Austrian School
with the Subjective Value Theory
* Eugen von Böhm-Bawerk [/people/eugen-bohm-bawerk/] -
Second-generation figure who solidified the School's foundations with
capital and interest theory
* Ludwig von Mises [/people/ludwig-von-mises/] - The giant who
completed the praxeological system
* Friedrich Hayek [/people/friedrich-hayek/] - Theorist of dispersed
knowledge and Spontaneous Order
* Henry Hazlitt [/people/henry-hazlitt/] - Journalist who brought
Austrian Economics to the general public
* Murray Rothbard [/people/murray-rothbard/] - Anarcho-capitalist who
integrated ethics and economics
